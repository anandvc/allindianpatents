Title of Invention

BIMETAL SUPPORTED CATALYST BASED ON PLATINUM OR SILVER ITS MANUFACTURING PROCESS AND ITS USE FOR ELECTROCHEMICAL CELLS
Abstract The invention relates to a stabilized bimetal supported catalyst based on platinum or silver, used in the electrodes for electrochemical cells. Accordingly the invention provides a stabilized bimetal catalyst comprising an electrically conductive support and a metallic deposited coating consisting of a first metal M1 chosen from platinum and silver and a second metal M2, which is different from the first metal M1 and is chosen from platinum, silver, gold, ruthenium, iridium, rhodium and osmium. The invention also provides a process for obtaining such a catalyst. The platinum- or silver-based bimetal catalysts of the present invention have the advantage of holding the mass activity, defined as the reduction current per unit mass of catalyst, constant over time or else of keeping the specific activity of the catalyst, i.e. the reduction current per unit area of active surface, constant.
Full Text The present invention relates to a stabilized bimetal supported catalyst based on platinum or silver, used in the electrodes for electrochemical cells.
More specifically, the invention relates to a bimetal supported catalyst based on platinum or silver, for use in the electrodes of an electrolysis cell having a membrane and an oxygen-reducing cathode which produces a solution of alkali metal hydroxide and of chlorine by the electrolysis of an aqueous solution of an alkali metal chloride (for example NaCl or KCl).
Such an electrolysis cell generally comprises a cation exchange membrane which divides the cell into an anode compartment and at least one cathode compartment in which a cathode is placed, the said cathode compartment being supplied with an oxygen-containing gas.
The anode and cathode reactions in such electrolytic processes using an oxygen-reducing cathode are:

In order to speed up the reactions occurring at the cathodes and to lower the reaction overvoltage in absolute value, catalysts are used, generally noble metals such as platinum, silver or gold, which are deposited on electrically conductive supports such as, especially, carbonaceous materials having a high specific surface area or metal substrates.
Preferably, platinum or silver is used, most particularly platinum since, although this is an expensive metal, it has the lowest value of cathode overpotential in absolute value.
It has now been observed, in particular during periods when electrolysis cells such as those mentioned above have stopped operating, that there is an agglomeration of platinum or silver particles on the support used. This has the effect of decreasing the "active" specific surface area of the catalyst, resulting in a loss of performance. In particular, an increase in the cell voltage is observed, resulting in an increase in the consumption of energy.
Tomantscheger et al. (J. Power Sources 1992, vol. 39 (1), pages 21-

41) have observed that when catalysts consisting of platinum particles supported on carbon are exposed to potassium that there is a significant agglomeration of the said platinum particles. The diameter of the Pt particles goes from 50 A to 200 A after approximately 48 hours exposure to potassium at 80Â°C.
Pataki et al (Electrochemical See, Meet., Toronto, Ont., May 12-17, 1985, Ext. Abstr. No. 659, pages 924-925) have shown that by pretreating a catalyst consisting of platinum particles supported on carbon with carbon monoxide (CO) there was a much smaller agglomeration of the said Pt particles when, as previously, the catalyst was exposed to potassium.
It has now surprisingly been found that it is possible to decrease, or even eliminate the agglomeration of platinum or silver particles of supported catalysts by simultaneously depositing, on the support, when preparing the catalyst, a first metal Ml chosen from platinum and silver and a second metal M2, different from the first and chosen from the group consisting of platinum, silver, gold, ruthenium, iridium, rhodium and osmium.
Accordingly the present invention provides a stabilized bimetal catalyst comprising an electrically conductive support and a deposited metal coating consisting of a first metal Ml chosen from platinum and silver and a second metal M2, which is different from the first metal Ml and is chosen from platinum, silver, gold, ruthenium, iridium, rhodium and osmium.
Preferably, M2 is platinum, silver or ruthenium.
According to the present invention, the mass ratio M1/M2 of the metals Ml and M2 of the deposited metal coating is generally at least 1 and preferably between 1 and 20.
According to the present invention, carbonaceous materials, such as graphite, furnace black, carbon black or carbon powder, having specific surface areas of at least 50 mVg and preferably between 100 and 600 mVg, are typically used as the electrically conductive support. These carbonaceous materials may be pretreated, especially in order to create functional groups on the surface of the carbonaceous material.
By way of illustration of such carbonaceous supports, mention may be

made of the graphite called TIMCAL HSAG-300 having a specific surface area of 360 mVg, Sibunit 5 which is a carbon having a specific surface area of 360 mVg, and the carbon VULCAN XC-72R which is a furnace black having a specific surface area of 300 mVg.
The bimetal catalyst of the present invention may be prepared by a co-reduction of mixtures of reducible metal salts using a process which comprises simultaneously impregnating, in a solvent medium, the electrically conductive support with a solution of a metal salt of the first metal Ml, chosen from platinum and silver, and with a solution of a metal salt of the second metal M2, which is different from the first metal Ml and is chosen from platinum, silver, gold, ruthenium, iridium, rhodium and osmium; evaporating to dryness, generally slowly, with stirring and with an inert-gas sparge, the suspension consisting of the solutions of the metal salts of the metals Ml and M2 and the carbonaceous support; drying the powder obtained, under atmospheric pressure, at a temperature of between 60Â°C and 80Â°C and then, under a reduced pressure, at a temperature of between 90Â°C and 110Â°C; subjecting the dried powder obtained to a stream of hydrogen, at temperatures ranging from 300Â°C to 600Â°C, achieved at a rate of temperature rise of between 0.2Â°C and 1.5Â°C per minute and preferably ranging from 0.2Â°C to 1Â°C per minute, and then stopping the heating or else in holding the temperature obtained for a time of at most 20 hours and preferably for a time of between 2 hours and 16 hours.
Preferably, alcoholic or aqueous/alcoholic solutions of the metal salts of the metals Ml and M2 are used. The preferred alcohol is ethanol. The concentrations by weight of the metal salts of the metals Ml and M2 of the said solutions may vary over a wide range. They may range from a few grams to a few tens of grams of salts per litre. They are calculated in such a way that, when the said solutions containing the salts of metals Ml and M2 are mixed, a mass ratio M1/M2, of the metals Ml and M2 to be simultaneously deposited on the support, at least 1, and preferably between 1 and 20, is obtained.
Next, having produced the mixture of the said solutions, a carbonaceous support and an impregnation solvent, such as benzene or toluene, generally in an amount of at least 10 ml of solvent per gram of support, are

introduced into the said mixture and then the suspension obtained is held for a few days at ambient temperature with stirring and with an inert-gas, typically nitrogen, sparge. This allows the solvents to evaporate and the support to be perfectly impregnated by the metal salts.
Next, the powder obtained is firstly dried at atmospheric pressure at a temperature of between 60Â°C and 80Â°C, preferably at a temperature close to 70Â°C, and then at a reduced pressure at a temperature of between 90Â°C and 110Â°C, preferably at a temperature close to 100Â°C.
At this stage, the support is impregnated by the salt of Ml and the salt of M2. Next, the cations are reduced in a stream of hydrogen.
The stability of the bimetal catalysts of the present invention which are thus obtained is tested. For this purpose, they are put into suspension in alkaline solutions at temperatures of between 60Â°C and 90Â°C and exposed to a stream of oxygen for several hours, or even several days.
The change in the size of the particles of Ml and in the metallic deposited coating consisting of Ml and M2 is monitored, before and after the treatment, by X-ray diffraction.
This method of analysis makes it possible to state that a stabilized bimetal supported catalyst is obtained.
This is because the particles of metal Ml no longer build up when they are deposited on a support simultaneously with particles of a metal M2, different from metal Ml.
The bimetal supported catalysts, stabilized according to the present invention, may be used for electrochemical cells.
These catalysts may especially be used in the production of electrodes for electrolysis cells having an oxygen-reducing cathode.
The platinum- or silver-based bimetal catalysts of the present invention have the advantage of holding the mass activity, defined as the reduction current per unit mass of catalyst, constant over time or else of keeping the specific activity of the catalyst, i.e. the reduction current per unit area of active surface, constant.

A suspension is prepared by mixing the catalyst powder, ultrapure water and ethanol. This suspension is then homogenized by ultrasonic stirring for 2 hours. A PTFE suspension is added to this mixture and the final mixture is again ultrasonically homogenized.
An end-piece made of glassy carbon is firstly polished with diamond paste (down to 1 nm) and ultrasonically rinsed, in succession, in acetone, ethanol and ultrapure water for 15 minutes. It is then dried in an oven. 10 Âµl of the final suspension are then deposited using a microsyringe onto the glassy carbon end-piece. It is then dried overnight at room temperature, and then for 15 minutes in an oven at 200Â°C in order to establish the mechanical integrity of the coating. The electrode thus obtained is fitted into the cell shown in Figure 1.
The cell is composed of a rotating-disc electrode (1) onto which the end-piece (2), prepared beforehand, is fitted, of a Luggin capillary (3) connected to the reference electrode (4), of a platinum counterelectrode (5) and of a sparger (6) for supplying oxygen. The rotating electrode (1), the reference electrode (4) and the counterelectrode (5) are connected to a potentiostat (not shown in Figure 1). Activity measurements:
The platinum active surface area of the electrode is determined from the trace of a cyclic voltammetry curve in (superpure) IM H^SO, medium in a nitrogen (argon) atmosphere over the hydrogen adsorption/desorption range. Next, the electrode is placed in a similar cell containing 1 mol/1 of sodium hydroxide made from superpure MERCK sodium hydroxide and ultrapure water. The temperature of the cell is fixed at 25Â°C. Oxygen is bubbled into the sodium hydroxide for several minutes before starting the tests so as to saturate the sodium hydroxide with dissolved oxygen. Next, a potential sweep between +0.1 V (Hg/HgO) and -0.6 V (Hg/HgO) is carried out at 1 mV/s and for various rotation speeds of the rotating electrode (500, 1000, 2000 and 4000 rpm) and the observed current for a potential of -60 mV (Hg/HgO) is recorded for the various electrode rotation speeds.
