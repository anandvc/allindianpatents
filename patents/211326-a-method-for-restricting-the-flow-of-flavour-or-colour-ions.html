5 The invention relates to the use of antifreeze proteins in ice confections. In particular the invention relates to the use of antifreeze proteins in ice confections to restrict the flow of flavors and/or colors.
10 Background to the Invention
It is a well-known problem that when an ice confection such as a water ice is consumed the flavor and colors is quickly sucked out of the product leaving essentially a block of ice 15 which is of limited enjoyment to eat.

comprised of a number of different components, each having a
different colors or flavor, the boundary between each
20 component is not sharp and distinct because of the flow of
the colour or flavour to a certain extent from one component
to another. Consequently it has not been possible to date
to provide an ice confection having thin components of a
different flavour or colour which remain distinct.

10 The inclusion of antifreeze proteins into ice confections results in the formation of a strong, close-packed continuous network of ice crystals within the ice confection.
15 By close-packed continuous network of ice crystals is meant that any given ice crystal is connected to at least one other ice crystal.
In unaerated ice confections which have been frozen with 20 agitation, the degree of network formation can be measured as contiguity.
Contiguity is defined as the ratio of the particle to particle interface area divided by the total interface area. 25 It is thus a measure of the degree of network formation of the particle phase. Example 2 shows a method for the measurement of contiguity.
