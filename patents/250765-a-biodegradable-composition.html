Title of Invention

"A BIODEGRADABLE COMPOSITION"
Abstract The invention relates to increasing the breathability of a biodegradable polymer film. The invention features a composition that includes biodegradable aliphatic-aromatic copolyester and plasticizer that includes polar groups, the composition exhibits a moisture vapor transmission rate of at least 650 g/m2/day.

BACKGROUND OF THE INVENTION
The invention relates to increasing the breathability of a biodegradable polymer film.
It is desirable for films that are used in disposable applications including, e.g., personal hygiene, to be breathable and to exhibit good "hand," i.e., softness. Breathabihty refers to the ability of moisture or water vapor to diffuse through a medium, e.g., a film or garment. The moisture vapor transmission rate of a film is generally related to the breathabihty of the film.
Various biodegradable polymers have been developed in an effort to enhance the disposal of waste generated by personal hygiene articles and disposable diapers in particular. These biodegradable polymers degrade under compost conditions into organic components such as carbon dioxide and water.
Some of these biodegradable polymers have high viscosity. As a result, it is difficult to process these biodegradable polymers under standard hot melt conditions and to coat thin films using these biodegradable polymers.
U.S. 5,625,029 (Hubbs et al.) discloses poly(3-hydroxyalkanoates) thermoplastic polymers that are described as being biodegradable, and blends of the poly(3-hydroxyalkanoates) with aliphatic-aromatic copolyester.
U.S. 5,70,344 (Edgington et al.) discloses a tackifymg resin that is based on polyester derived from 2-propanoic acid and is described as being biodegradable and compostable. Edgington et al. also disclose biodegradable compostable thermoplastic resins. Edgington et al disclose that the tackifying resin can be combined with biodegradable and compostable polymer or resins to form adhesive compositions including, e.g., hot melt adhesives and hot melt pressure sensitive adhesives. Edgington et al. further disclose small molecule compostable plasticizers that can be combined with the compostable thermoplastic resins and tackifying resins.
U.S. 5,988,505 (Brink) discloses compositions that include thermoplastic elastomers and microporous inorganic fillers. The film of which is described as being capable of exhibiting a moisture vapor transmission rate of at least 200 g mil/m2/day. Brink discloses thermoplastic elastomers that include copolyesters, polyamides, and polyurethanes. Examples of specifically disclosed copolyesters include poly(tetramtheylene gluatarate-co-therephthalate-co-diglycolate, poly(tetramethylene glutarate-co-terephthalate, polyethylene succinate-co-terephthalate), poly(tetramethylene succinate-co-terephthalate), and poly(tetramethylene adipate-co-terephthalate.
EP 0 963,837 Al, EP 0 963 760 Al and EP 0 964 026 Al describe thermoplastic compositions that include thermoplastic polymer and plasticizer, and are capable of forming moisture vapor permeable films.

DETAILED DESCRIPTION
The composition includes biodegradable aliphatic-aromatic copolyester and plasticizer. The composition is capable of forming a breathable film that preferably exhibits a moisture vapor transmission rate (MVTR) of at least 400 g/m2/day, more preferably at least about 1000 g/m2/day, more preferably at least about 2000 g/m2/day. The addition of a plasticizer to a biodegradable aliphatic-aromatic copolyester having
relatively low MVTR surprisingly improves the MVTR of the copolyester. The MVTR of the composition is detennined according to the MVTR Test Method.
The composition can be formulated to be biodegradable.
The biodegradable aliphatic-aromatic copolyesters break down under compost conditions over a verifiable period of time into organic components including, e.g., carbon dioxide and water. The coating device (3) is located at a distance from the substrate (1) to be coated, the distance ranging from about 0.5 mm to about 20 mm depending on the properties of the composition (6) being coated. An optional second substrate (4) can be adhered to the coated surface of the first substrate (1) by means of a nip roll (5). When adherence to a second substrate (4) is intended, it is often preferred to position the nip roll (5) closer to the coating device (3) at a distance of about 25 cm from the coating device (3).
The composition (6) exits the coating device (3) in the form of a continuous film (8). When the composition (6) leaves the coating device (3) in the form of a film (8), it does not contact the substrate (1) immediately. Instead, it travels for a distance as a continuous film suspended above the substrate (1) without touching the substrate (1). The distance between the coating device and the substrate ranges from about 0.5 mm to about 20 mm. The distance is largely influenced by the viscosity of the composition being coated. Preferably the continuous film (8) travels in a substantially horizontal direction to contact the first substrate (1), i.e., the continuous film is directed in a horizontal direction and the substrate to be coated is directed in a vertical direction.
