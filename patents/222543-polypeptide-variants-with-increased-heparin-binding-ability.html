Polypeptide Variants with Increased Heparin-Binding Ability
The present invention pertains to polypeptide variants with increased heparin-binding ability. Furthermore the present invention pertains to nucleic acid molecules encoding these variants, the host cells containing the nucleic acid molecules, and the processes used to produce polypeptide variants and recombinant polypeptide variants. Also, the present invention pertains to the use of polypeptide variants to stimulate chondrogenesis, osteogenesis, and wound healing, and to treat inflammation and cancer. In addition, the present invention pertains to osteoinductive compositions containing these variants.
Numerous biological factors influence the development and regeneration of cells, tissues, and organs of the human and animal body. Even today, many of these factors are still unknown, and the processes they control are not yet completely understood. Bone formation (osteogenesis) is a process that is as yet not completely understood; it can be broken down into numerous consecutive single processes, such as, for example, chemotaxis, mitosis, and differentiation. Chemotaxis, in this context, means the direct migration of cells in response to a chemical gradient of signal connections that are released from the insoluble demineralized bone matrix, consisting primarily of type I Insoluble collagen. Type I insoluble collagen binds plasma fibronectin which itself contains domains that bind collagen, fibrin, and heparin.
There are principally two main groups of biologically active factors that control osteogenesis, those that are systemically active and those that are locally active.
The group of systemically active factors includes, for example, the two hormones PTH (parathyroid hormone) and 1,25-dihydroxy-vitamin D, which regulate endogenic calcium levels. An additional hormone involved in osteogenesis is calcitonin, which prevents bone resorption.

Estrogens, androgens, growth hormones, insulin-like growth factor (IGF), thyroid hormones, and glucocorticoids, for example, are also among the systemic hormones that regulate osteogenesis.
The group of locally active factors encompasses
(i) cytokines that effect bone decomposition such as IL-1, tumor necrosis factor (TNF), IL-6, IL-11, and ODF (osteoclastic differentiation factor, TRANCE);
(ii) cytokines that prevent bone decomposition: IL-4, IL-13, IL-18, IFN (interferon), OPG (osteoprotegerin), and IL-1ra (interleukin-1-receptor antagonist);
(iii) colony-stimulating factors: M-CSF (macrophage-colony stimulating factor) and GM-CSF (granulocyte-macrophage stimulating factor);
(iv) prostaglandins, leukotrienes, and nitrogen monoxide; and
(v) growth factors: IGF (insulin-like growth factor), proteins from the
decapentaplegic-Vg-related (DVR) family including the proteins of the TGF-B (transforming growth factor (i) superfamily, which encompasses the proteins of the activin/inhibin family, MIS (Mullerian inhibitory substance), GDF (growth/differentiation factor), nodal, and dorsalin; FGF (fibroblast growth factor), PDGF (platelet-derived growth factor), and PTHrP (PTH-related protein; parathyroid hormone-related protein).
In the context of the present invention, the TGF-li superfamily is of particular interest.
At this point in time, the TGF-S superfamily comprises more than twenty proteins, not counting the suspected orthologs in various organisms. Along with the TGF-(is, the BMPs (bone morphogenetic proteins), the GDFs (growth differentiation factors) inhibins/activins, and other proteins (Kingsley, 1994) belong to the superfamily. They all consist of two sub-units that are almost always homodimers of two identical monomers, covalently linked by a disulfide bridge.

One characterizing feature of all proteins of the TGF-(i superfamily structurally studied up to the present is the "TGF-S/BMP structure", which consists of a cysteine knot, an a-helix, and at least four 13-strands per monomer, resulting in a typical arrangement of the monomers in the dimer (McDonald and Hendrickson, 1993). The amino acid sequences can vary considerably and sometimes resemble each other with fewer than 40% identical positions. The "loop" areas as well as the N-terminal sequences are particularly variable. Nonetheless, a surprising degree of conservation of function and structure has been shown for the evolution of all the members of the TGF-B superfamily. They all contain a structural element designated as the "cysteine knot" that consists of three disulfide bridges which are conserved and identically arranged in all the proteins of the TGF-S superfamily. Apart from the TGF-(Ss, representative members that have been particularly well studied are the individual members of the BMPs such as BMP-2 and BMP-7, and members of the GDFs such as GDF-5, which can turn on the development and regeneration of bones and cartilage tissue. Thus for example, BMP-2 has been shown to display osteoinductive properties in heterotopic as well as orthotopic implants.
The proteins of the TGF-(l superfamily are synthesized within the cell initially as one large proprotein from which the mature protein is created by proteolysis. It seems that after an ArgXXArg recognition sequence the proprotein is cleaved off so that a C-terminal sequence of about 100 to 140 amino acid residues, which represent the mature protein, is released.
For signal transduction the factors belonging to the TGF-(3 superfamily bind to the extra¬cellular domains of two types of membrane receptors of their target cells, e.g. inducible bone marrow stem cells. The type 2 sub-unit contains within its cytoplasmatic part a protein serine kinase, which upon ligand binding within the type 1 sub-unit phosphorylates serine residues. This also activates within the type 1 sub-unit a protein serine kinase, which can phosphorylate and activate intracellular signal proteins such as SMADs. There are indications that sub-units of both type 1 and type 2 exist as dimmers. Recently, the crystal structure of the extracellular domain of the type 2 sub-unit of the

activin receptor (Act-RII) was determined. BMP-2, which is among the best studied proteins of the TGF-S family, binds to the receptor sub-units via the structurally highly conserved domain after the first cysteine of the cysteine knot. The highly variable N-terminal sequence before the first cysteine of the cysteine knot does not directly interact in BMP-2 with the receptor sub-units. The interaction of proteins of the TGF-li superfamily with type 1 and type 2 receptor sub-units is partly promiscuous and the specifities are overlapping. It is not yet clear to what extent there is common usage of receptor sub-units or to what extent there are differences in the mechanism of receptor activation. In particular, current literature does not unequivocally reveal which structural elements determine the specificity of a protein, e.g. for osteoinductive activity.
The members of the TGF-(5 superfamily can interact with a number of other proteins other than the receptor sub-units. Thus, the activity of the members can be modulated or inhibited. Fetuin/a2-HS glycoprotein and a peptide derived from it (TRH1) bind BMP-2 and TGF-(i, with BMP-2 being bound with higher affinity. This binding competes with the binding at the receptor. Mammalian noggin protein binds BMP-2 with a high affinity in competition with the receptor. As shown in Xenopus laevis oocytes, chordin acts as an inhibitor for BMP-4. Follistatin binds with high affinity to activin and BMP-7. BMP-2 has been proven to be capable of binding to heparin.
The therapeutic potential of the members of the TGF-li superfamily is apparent due to their physiological significance. Recombinantly produced proteins are of particular interest, because they can be obtained in large quantities. Moreover, the nucleic acids that encode them are potential agents in gene therapy.
Thus, there is a general interest in members of the TGF-(i superfamily and their variants with altered biological properties. Kubler et al. (1999) describe a BMP analog EHBMP-2 the primary structure of which differs from that of naturally occurring human BMP-2 in that the first twelve amino acids, which are considered responsible for the strong heparin binding of BMP-2, are replaced bv the first thirteen amino acids of human

interleukin-2. This genetically altered BMP-2 analog was recombinantly expressed in E. coli. EHBMP-2 reveals a negligible affinity to heparin and a higher biological activity in various cell cultures, i.e. in vitro. In comparing the in vivo activity of the variant with that of natural BMP-2, it was shown that in mouse at BMP-2 concentrations starting from 4 |jm a heterotopic bone induction was produced in nearly all samples, whereas it took an amount of about 40 pm of EHBMP-2 to achieve the same effect. Furthermore, it was found that the resulting extent of new bone formation at the same protein concentrations was significantly greater in natural BMP-2 than in its BMP analog, EHBMP-2.

Below, a few terms will be explained in more detail to clarify how they are to be understood in the context of the present application.

The term "polypeptide" as it is used below in the description, comprises peptides or proteins consisting of 5 or more amino acids and having at least one biological activity. Furthermore, the term comprises biologically active fragments of polypeptides, and their mutants and fusion proteins.
"Polypeptide variant with increased heparin-binding ability" means that the polypeptide variant shows increased ability to bind heparin greater than that of the unaltered polypeptide. Generally, the ability to bind heparin can be measured by, for example, plasma resonance analysis with the aid of a heparin coated carrier. The individual experimental conditions are described for example in Ruppert et al., 1996.
In vivo efficiency is understood to mean the extent of the intended effect at the target site. The in vivo efficiency is determined by the biological activity of the protein in conjunction with the availability of the protein at the target site, in the final analyis, it is measured as the effect at the target site. Established methods may be used to measure the effect. In the case of BMP-2 variants, the induction of ectopic bone formation, as described in KCibler & Urist, 1991, and Kubler et a!., 1999, has turned out to be useful.
A biologically active polypeptide that modulates growth and/or the differentiation of cells is called a "growth factor".

Surprisingly, it has been found that a polypeptide variant of this invention showed a higher heparin-binding ability than the unaltered polypeptide in that at least one oligopeptide containing an amino acid sequence according to the invention is added to and/or inserted into the polypeptide, and/or replaces some of the amino acids as polypeptides. It could be shown that in comparison to the unaltered polypeptide the amount of the variants bound to heparin was greater and that there was also less dissociation of the variants from heparin. Because heparin-like structures are integral components of the bone structure, the polypeptide variants of the invention also exhibit an increased binding capacity for bone structures. This property is especially important for regenerative processes in the bone structure, where in this case the polypeptides concerned participate in bone morphogenesis (osteogenesis). It could be shown that the polypeptide variants of the invention increased heparin-binding ability by a factor of 30, but at least by a factor of 10. The increased heparin-binding ability in turn led to a dramatically increased in vivo efficiency, as demonstrated for example in figure 8.
As mentioned above, the effect of the polypeptides participating in osteogenesis is imparted by receptors that are located, inter alia, on the surface of inducible bone marrow stromal stem cells. The latter have the ability to form bone cells upon the influence of an inductive signal from morphogenetic proteins or demineralized bone matrix. Without wanting to be bound to this theory, it is assumed that the increase in heparin-binding ability of polypeptides, particularly of osteoinductive polypeptides results in an increase of the local concentration of osteogenetic proteins in the immediate vicinity of the location of osteogenesis.
However, because heparin competes with the cellular receptor of the polypeptide for the polypeptide variant, it must first be assumed that the increased heparin-binding ability of

the polypeptide variant will decrease the amount of polypeptide available to the polypeptide-specific receptor and thus lead to a decrease in signal transduction.
Surprisingly, it has been found that the biological availability of the polypeptide variant is not affected by the simultaneous increase in heparin-binding ability, but rather that the increased heparin-binding ability correlates with an increase in the local concentration of polypeptide available for the specific receptor.
Furthermore, it has surprisingly been found that the bone formed by the polypeptide variant in vivo is of better quality than that formed by the unaltered polypeptide.

In general, an increased binding ability to extra-cellular matrix structures and cell surfaces has also been observed. Therapeutically active polypeptide variants can therefore be used to prevent and/or treat diseases involving or affecting the extracellular matrix or cell surfaces.
If the polypeptides underlying the polypeptide variants belong to the DVR family, they will be suitable to promote bone growth and bone repair. GDF-5 participation in cartilage formation has been described and it is postulated for the development of ligaments. Thus, GDF-5 derived polypeptide variants are suitable for repairing cartilage and ligaments. BMP-7/0P-1 suppresses the production of GDF-5, which possibly represents a mechanism to balance bone and cartilage formation. Thus, polypeptide variants derived from BMP-7 demonstrate regulatory properties in the context of bone and cartilage formation.
BMP-7 participates in kidney and eye development, BMP-6 in skin development, and BMP-2 in heart development. Thus, according to this invention, polypeptide variants derived from BMP can be used to regulate kidney, eye, skin, and heart development.
Furthermore, GDF-5 induces angiogenesis. Therefore, GDF-5 derived polypeptide variants could be important in stimulating angiogenesis.
If the polypeptides of the polypeptide variants belong to the TGF-li family, the therapeutically efficient polypeptide variants are suitable for immune suppression, inflammation inhibition, and for stimulation of bone and cartilage formation. Due to increased depositing of extra-cellular matrix components such as collagens, fibronectins, glycosaminoglycans and proteoglycans, they are useful in wound healing.

Furthermore, polypeptide variants based on TGF-B are useful to prevent detachment of the retina. Also, they can be advantageously applied to combat oral mucositis, a side effect of tumor chemotherapy. Because of the general cell growth inhibiting effect of TGF-S, they can be used to suppress cancer cells such as for example breast cancer cells.
The involvement of the pituitary hormones, activins and inhibins in modulating the female menstrual cycle is known. Activin induces and inhibin inhibits synthesis of the pituitary hormones FSH and LH. Thus, it is suggested to use polypeptide variants according to the invention to regulate the female menstrual cycle.
Inhibins suppress the development of gonadal tumors. Inhibin-derived polypeptide variants could therefore be applied to prevent and treat gonadal tumors.
Activins are presumed to participate in wound healing. The invention thus encompasses the use of activin-derived polypeptide variants to produce drugs for wound healing.
Activins may be involved in the development and conversion of cartilage to bone. Thus, the activin-derived polypeptide variants may be suited to regulate growth of bone and cartilage.
Activin promotes expansion of blast-forming sub-units and colony-forming sub-units during hematopoiesis, while inhibin inhibits these functions. Therefore, the use of polypeptide variants derived from these proteins is suggested for the regulation of hematopoiesis.
Further particularly important polypeptides that may serve as bases for polypeptide variants are MIS and GDNF (glial cell line derived neurotrophic factor), both of which participate in embryonic development.

In another embodiment, the invention provides a matrix for osteoinduction comprising at least one polypeptide variant and a carrier. Furthermore, an in vitro process is suggested for osteoinduction, using the osteoinductive matrix.
The prior art suggests osteoinductive matrices that contain growth factors. For example, a collagen matrix produced by Integra Life Sciences Corp., which comprises recombinant human BMP-2, is being developed (Hollinger et al., 1998). Also, the company Sofamor-Danek is developing titanium cages containing recombinant human BMP-2. Finally, there is a device named "NOVOS™" manufactured by the company Creative Biomolecules, now being further developed by the company Stryker, that contains osteoinductive type-1 bone collagen and recombinant OP-1 (BMP-7). This device is currently in clinical phase lii in the United States of America. Applications include treatment of orthopedic traumas, maxillofacial repairs, and avascular necroses. Also, the use of BMP-7 is suggested for treatment of cartilage damage, kidney failure, brain damage, and bone marrow damage, heart attacks and osteoporosis.
Typically, a matrix material is provided with osteoinductive growth factors, and then the matrix is surgically implanted. The matrix, in this case, is not only a earner for the growth factor, but also confers physical stability and prevents invasion of soft tissue into the location of bone defects. The addition of growth factors is to speed the replacement of the implant by new bone.
One major problem with the known types of matrices, however, is that the proteins contained in the matrix very rapidly leave the site of application. Because bone repair is a relatively slow process even in the presence of growth factors, the short retention time of the added protein has a limiting effect.
Since the biological effect of osteoinductive growth factors is to induce immigration of pluripotent mesenchymal cells to form chondro precursor cells and osteo precursor cells, and to stimulate their activity, it is necessary to localize the growth factors to the

repair site. Growth factors that diffuse away from the repair site, on the one hand, fail in their intended therapeutic purpose, and on the other hand, also pose a threat of causing ectopic bone formation.
The polypeptide variants according to the invention are characterized by an increased heparin-binding ability over known polypeptides, which property can be exploited by using heparin or heparin-like structures as matrix material or as material applied to the matrix to prevent rapid diffusion of polypeptide variants away from the carrier. The polypeptide variants thus remain localized at the site of application. In a preferred embodiment, the carrier should consist of heparin, hydroxyapatite, hyaluronic acid, synthetic polymers, or collagen. The carrier materials may or may not be resorbable.
The prior art discloses various procedures to produce pharmaceutically acceptable matrices. Examples of pharmaceutically acceptable matrices are described in Wagner et al., 1996, and Fischgrund et al., 1997. The matrix may take the form of a block, gel, fleece, sphere, or granules.
Distribution of polypeptide variants within the matrix may or may not be homogenous, but a homogenous distribution would be preferable. The distribution of polypeptide variants may be advantageously configured, depending on the size of the defect or the duration of the healing process. The polypeptide variant concentration within the carrier should range from about 100 ftg /cm^ to about 2 mg/cm^, preferably from 250 /Jgr/cm^ to 750 l>g/cm^, and particularly preferably from 450 f-igycm^ to 550 ^tg/cm^. As a rule, a concentration of about 500 pg/cm^ is used.
According to the invention, the osteoinductive matrix may be used to treat orthopedic trauma, maxillofacial repairs, and avascular necroses. Furthermore, it may be recommended for the prevention and treatment of damaged cartilage, kidney failure, brain damage, bone marrow damage, heart attacks, and osteoporosis.

The osteoinductive matrix according to the invention also offers the advantage when using recombinantly produced polypeptide variants that it is free of contamination, e.g. viruses that are observed from time to time with growth factors of animal origin.
The osteoinductive matrix according to the invention may be used in vitro to enable colonization in tissue culture of osteoblasts and/or chondroblasts. The osteoinductive matrices prepared in this way can then be implanted in patients autologously or heterologously by surgical methods (Kubler et al., 1997; Kubler et al., 1998; Chen et al., 1998).

Methods
Measurement of Heparin Binding
To measure heparin-binding, Heparin 6000 was aminobiotinylated (Mach et al., 1993) and fixed to a streptavidin-coated biosensor CMS (Pharmacia Biosensor AB). The binding of polypeptides and polypeptide variants according to the invention with increased heparin-binding ability to the heparin-doped biosensor was measured with the aid of a BIA2000 instrument. The individual experimental conditions have been described (Ruppert et al., 1996).
Measurement of Bindino to Receptor BMPR-IA
To measure the binding, the ectodomain of the receptor BMPR-IA was aminobiotinylated (Shen et a!., 1996J and fixed to a streptavidin-coated biosensor CMS (Pharmacia Biosensor AB). The binding of polypeptides and the polypeptide variants according to the invention to the receptor-doped biosensor was measured with the aid of a BIA2000 instrument.

The following cell culture system was used to measure the biological activity: Cells were isolated from limb buds of chicken embryos, and used for the measurement of the BMP-dependent incorporation of ^S04 in proteoglycans (Ruppert et a!., 1996). Example 3: Studies on in vivo Efficiency of Polypeptide Variants
The method chosen here was the induction of ectopic bones in the thigh muscle of the mouse. Ectopic bone formation means that bone is formed de novo in foreign tissue and without contact to the skeletal system. The advantage of this test system comes from its high stringency. Because there is no contact to any skeletal bone, regenerative bone healing processes do not play any roll. Thus, false positive test results are ruled out such as those that may result from bone damage occurring during surgery.
The method to induce ectopic bone formation in ICR mice is described in detail in Kubler and Urist, 1991.
BMP-2 and variant T3 were mixed with bovine serum albumin as carrier in various concentrations, and implanted into the quadriceps femoris muscles of ICR mice. After three weeks, the newly formed bone material was characterized by X-ray imaging and histological examinations.
The implantation of bovine serum albumin without BMP (control) resulted in no detectable bone formation. No symptoms of inflammation or other injurious side effects were observed in the case of BMP or T3. Fig. 7 indicates that BMP-2 could induce the formation of bone matrix and bone marrow. Fig. 8 shows the correlative treatment with T3. In comparison to the result with BMP-2, it becomes apparent for T3 that the ratio of bone matrix to bone marrow is strongly shifted to a high amount of bone matrix.
Fig. 9 shows an X-ray image of an ossicle formed by T3 implantation. The size of the ossicle does not significantly differ from that of ossicles resulting from conventional BMP treatment in the same test system.
Fig. 10 shows the histological view of an ossicle that was induced by treatment with T3. The staining was performed with Masson-Trichrome which allows one to distinguish the

stages of differentiation of bone tissue. The color red indicates completely differentiated bones, turquoise stained areas are in the process of ossification. In some parts, one can make out round white cells within the turquoise stained tissue; these are residual parts of cartilage-forming chondrocytes, because BMP-induced bone formation involves endochondral ossification (transient cartilage formation).
