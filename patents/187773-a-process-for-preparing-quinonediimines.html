The class of cyclic enones is well known in organic chemistry. Best known examples of cyclic enones are quinones such as, for example, the benzoquincnes, naphthoquinones, anthraquinones, phenanthraquinor.es, and the like. l,4~3enaoquinone is commonly referred to as quinone. Quinones are generally brightly colored compounds and have versatile applications in chemical synthesis, biological uses, as redox materials, as well as in industry. There are several review articles on the chemistry and applications of quinones including, for example, Kirk-Othmsr Encyclopedia of Chemlca.1 Technology, Third ed., Vol. 19, pages 572-605, John Wiley & Sons, New York, 1982.
The synthesis of quinones is well documented. See, for example, J. Cason, SyntheBis of Beazcquinones by

Oxidation, in Organic Synthesis, Vol. IV, page 305, John Wiley & Sons, New York (194S) . Quinones generally are prepared by oxidizing the appropriately disubstituted aromatic hydrocarbon derivatives/ the substituents being hydroxyl or amino groups in the ortho or para positions. i,4-Benzoquincne, for example, can be made from the oxidation of hydroquinone, p-aminophenol or p-phenylenediamine, or from guinic acid. The reagents generally used for the oxidation are dichromate/sulfuric acid mixture, ferric chloride, silver (II) oxide or eerie ammonium nitrate. In these cases, oxidation of the aminaaromatic compound is accompanied by hydrolysis to the corresponding quincne. Some processes may take several hours for completion of the reaction.
Thus, some of the prior art processes utilize a catalytic agent to achieve an acceptable reaction rate while other processes proceed without catalysts. The process according to the present invention utilizes a hypochlorite reagent which provides extremely high conversion, high selectivity, and fast reaction rates to prepare the quinor.ediimine,
A prior art process which utilizes a catalyst in the preparation of a quinoneimine compound is disclosed by Desmurs, et al. in U.S. Patent No- 5,189,218. The process of Desmurs, et al., which converts N-(4-hydroxyphenyl)aniline into JT-pher.ylbenzoquinone-imine, utilizes a manganese, copper, cobalt, and/or nickel compound as a catalyst in an oxidation type reaction.
The above process of Desmurs, et al., which uses a metal catalytic component, along with any other

processes which utilize a metal catalyst, have several drawbacks. Not only are the metal catalysts relatively expensive/ they raise important environmental concerns. For example, effluent streams and products can be contaminated by the metals. Further, recovery of the catalyst for reuse can be prohibitively expensive.
Other processes are known which use oxidizing agents to convert phenylenediair.ines into their corresponding quinor.ediiroines. For example, Â£P 7oa,oai (Bernhardt et al) , which describes the conversion of phenylenediamines to phenylenediiminee by oxidation of the diamine in an alkali/alcoholic solution, gives a general description of such processes in its background. The EP y081 process suffers from various disadvantages including long reaction times and low yields. Additional oxidation conversion processes are described by Wheeler in "J.s. Patent No. 5,118,807 and by Haas et al, in E? 708,080. However, the use of a hypochlorite as an oxidizing agent in the conversion of diamino compounds to give highly selective yields of diiminc compounds has not heretofore been suggested.
As such, the current invention is based on the problem of providing a simple and economic process for the preparation of N,N'-disubstituted guinonediimines in high yields and with high purity.

It has been discovered that phenylenediamine compounds can be converted with extremely high selectivity into the corresponding quinonadiimine by reaction of the diamine with a hypochlorite oxidant. Conditions are revealed in which nearly quantitative yields have been obtained.
In contrast to prior art, an advantage of the present invention is that the conversion of phenylenediamine to the corresponding guinonediimine is nearly quantitative. Thus, very little waste material remains upon completion of the reaction.
Another advantage comes from the use of the hypochlorite oxidizing agent. The hypochlorite oxidizing agent avoids the drawbacks associated with metal catalysts which include high cost, product contamination and environmental waste concerns.
An addtionai advantage is that the hypochlorite oxidizing agents, as set forth herein, provide an extremely high conversion, high selectivity and faster more complete reaction compared to prior art processes.
