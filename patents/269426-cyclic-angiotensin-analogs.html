The octapeptide angiotensin-II (Asp-Arg-Val-Tyr-Ile-His-Pro-Phe), also referred to as Angll or Ang(l-8) is one of the oldest peptide hormones, known for its multipUcity of biological actions related to endocrine or connected to the central and peripheral nervous system.
Ang(l-8) has multiple physiologic effects that regulate vascular tone, hormone secretion, tissue growth and neural activity. It has systemic and local effects, favoring cell powth and differentiation through four types of receptors. It may be applied in tissue repair and development. Antagonists of Ang(l-8) can be applied in antitumor action by inhibition of angiogenesia (Escobar et al 2004 Current Vascular Pharmacology 2, 385-399). Receptors of the renin angiotensin system (RAS) may be involved in the differentiation of stem cells (Ozturk et al 2004 Medical hypotheses 63, 866-874). Ang(l-8) I receptor blockers are widely used for heart and renal failure and hypertension.
Ang(l-8) is a potent pressor agent, which has a vital role in the regulation of blood pressure, in the conservation of total blood volume and salt homeostasis. Furthermore, it is involved in the release of alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH), cell growth and the stimulation of the sympathetic i system. Several antagonists of Ang(l-8) are efficient antipressor agents. Inadequate functioning of the renin-angiotensin system contributes substantially to the development of hypertension and cardiovascular and renal

pathology (including left ventricular hypertrophy, structural alternations of the vasculature, neointima formation, nephrosclerosis, etc.).
