BACKGROUND AND PRIOR ART
In recent years there has been an ever increasing choice for consumers who wish to quench their' thirst with ready made
10 beverages. Many of those are now turning from the well known
soft drinks to tea based beverages, be those carbonated or still, and the "natural" refreshment they can provide.
Tea contains a complex combination of enzymes, biochemical
15 intermediates and structural elements normally associated with plant growth and photosynthesis. There are also many natural substances that give tea its unique taste, astringency, aroma and colour. Many of these are produced by the oxidation reactions that occur during the so-called fermentation stage of black tea
20 manufacture. Tea production has long been driven by traditional processing methods with only a fundamental understanding of the chemistry that is involved. As a consequence manufacturers have discovered making ambient stable tea based beverages at the volumes required to compete with more traditional soft drinks is
25 not simply a matter of flavouring a soft drink with tea.
The flavour of a tea based beverage and its stability rely on the stability of the beverage as a whole. It is therefore critical to preserve the quality of the beverage. The yeamolds
30 that can grow in tea based beverages and other soft drinks can be killed or controlled by heat treatment or by use of preservatives. Some tea based beverages are therefore

Potassium sorbate is well known preservative. It is a mold and yeast inhibitor and one of the few legally permitted preservatives of soft drinks and fruit juices. It has been

Unfortunately even moderate levels of sorbic or benzoic acid can seriously affect the flavour of a tea based beverage. Adding a
25 strong flavour such as lemon can offset the preservative taste. However consumers are keen to experience other flavours, often more delicate flavours. Furthermore, some of those consumers that were drawn to tea based products as a more healthy and natural alternative to soft drinks would reduce their intake of

10 DETAILED DESCRIPTION OF THE INVENTION The beverage
The term "tea based beverage" describes a beverage that contains 15 the solid extracts of leaf material from Camellia sinensis,
Camellia assamica, or Aspalathus linearis. The leaves may have subjected to a so-called "fermentation" step wherein they are oxidised by certain endogenous enzymes that are released during the early stages of "black tea" manufacture. This oxidation may
20 even be supplemented by the action of exogenous enzymes such as oxidases, laccases and peroxidases. Alternatively the leaves may have been partially fermented ("oolong" tea) or substantially unfermented ("green tea") . The tea may be added to the beverage in various forms including an extract, a concentrate, a powder
25 as granules.
Adding tea to media often increases the risk of microbial spoilage. This is probably because tea provides nutrients for microbial growth. Most microbes that can typically grow in te

10 At low concentrations, such 0.01 to 3%, tea acts as a nutrient that enhances the potential for microbial spoilage. This is unexpected given the known antibacterial and antiviral properties of tea. It is not until one exceeds a concentration of 3% that tea begins to suppress the growth of yeasts and molds.

The polyphosphate
The polyphosphate of the present invention is one having the formula M- C-MP04-]n-M, where n is from 5 to 22 and M is
10 independently selected from the sodium and potassium atoms. Preferably n is from 7 to 20, or even from 10 to 20.
It has been found that in tea-based beverages, a concentration above 280 ppm has a negative taste effect. Therefore the level
15 of polyphosphate is from 100 to 280ppm. When combined with a preservative system, this level also gives good shelf life. Preferably the level of polyphosphate is from 100 to 250ppm.
The preservative system 20
The preservative system of the present invention comprises very low levels of both potassium sorbate and sodium benzoate. Specifically, both are present at levels of from 100 to 300 ppm,
preferably from 100 to 250ppm, more preferably from 100 to 200 25 ppm. This ensures that no negative taste effects are observed.
